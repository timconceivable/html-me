Childhood

I remember drawing a lot as a child. When I wasn't drawing, I was sculpting with Play-Dough or building with Legos. When I wasn't doing either of those, I was probably watching cartoons or playing with action figures (G.I. Joe, Transformers, He-Man, etc).
I drew a lot though. I started off like most do. Stick figures, then tanks, monster trucks, and other weird stuff boys are often into at an early age. I always did well in art class, and got a lot of support from my art teachers and my parents.

I remember clearly wanting to be an animator at Disney when I grew up when I was in elementary school. I loved Disney cartoons and films, and did a report on Walt Disney at some point. Then in fifth grade, my friends got into comic books, and I was instantly hooked as well. From then on, I wanted to draw comics. I dreamed of working for Marvel for a long time, and then maybe creating my own characters and book for Image Comics. I began copying images from comic books, and then start creating my own characters.
Around sixth grade, I went on a trip with my family to Florida and Disney World. On the way back we visited my mother's aunt and uncle in Savannah, GA. My great uncle saw that I liked to draw, and he just happened to have a copy of 'How To Draw Comics The Marvel Way', and it forever changed my life. My mother had gotten me a great book on cartooning prior to that (Preston Blair's 'Animation'), but 'The Marvel Way' gave me a whole new prespective on drawing.

High School

By the time I was in high school, I was drawing every day. In class, out of class; it didn't matter. I doodled on my homework assignments and on scrap paper. Drawing actually helped me focus on lectures.
I took art classes every semester through high school: drawing, painting, sculpture, graphic design, printmaking, and AP art as well. I was not the best student though. As much as I loved art, I did not like doing assignments. I actually failed my sculpture class because I just stopped being interested in the work, and I had to drop AP art after a semester because I wasn't finishing my projects. Perfectionism got the best of me, and it became a problem I have struggled with my entire life since.

Despite that, I kept drawing. I contributed sketches and short stories to a school creative writing publication. I had my mind set on studying art in college as well, and I applied to a few art colleges and got accepted. I chose to go to Savannah College of Art and Design because 1. it was far away from home, and I liked the city; and 2. they had a program in Sequential Art (comics).

Closing

I have certainly felt lost for a long time, but I'm happy to have had the experiences I have had. It's been an intersting journey, and I think that can only help make my creative output more interesting. I am also really happy to have finally discovered programming, and that I can make video games. I was focused on art for so long that it never occurred to me that I could do this kind of thing.
Life is full of surprises.
